# Coaching Methodology Reference

This document provides the evidence base and technique library for the sleep
coach skill. Read it before the first session or when adapting approach for a
user who is struggling.

## 1. Why Bedtime Habits Are Hard

Bedtime is a uniquely difficult habit target for several reasons the coach
should internalise:

- **Revenge bedtime procrastination**: After a long day, nighttime feels like
  the only "free" time. The user is trading future energy for present
  autonomy. The coach must respect this — dismissing it feels invalidating.
- **Low executive function at night**: Willpower is a depleted resource by
  evening. Any plan that relies on "just deciding to go to bed" will fail.
  The emphasis must be on environment design and pre-commitment earlier in
  the day.
- **Social and digital pull**: Friends, feeds, and content are maximally
  engaging at night. Competing with dopamine-optimised systems requires
  structural intervention, not motivation speeches.
- **Delayed feedback**: Going to bed earlier tonight does not feel rewarding
  tonight — the payoff is tomorrow morning. The coach bridges this gap by
  making the session itself a small reward (connection, reflection, progress
  tracking).

## 2. Core Frameworks

### Implementation Intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999)

"When [situation X], I will [behaviour Y]."

Implementation intentions roughly double follow-through rates compared to
raw goal intentions. The coach should help the user form one per session.

Good: "When my 9:30 PM alarm goes off, I will put my phone on the charger
in the kitchen."

Bad: "I'll try to go to bed earlier tonight."

### Habit Stacking (Clear, 2018)

"After [current habit], I will [new habit]."

Anchor the bedtime wind-down to something the user already does reliably.
Example: "After I brush my teeth, I will set out clothes for tomorrow and
get into bed."

### Identity-Based Habits (Clear, 2018)

Frame the change as identity, not outcome. Instead of "I want to go to bed
by 10", encourage "I'm becoming someone who respects their energy." Each
completed session is a vote for the new identity.

The coach's closing line should reinforce this: "You showed up again tonight.
That's what an early riser does."

### The Two-Minute Rule (Allen / Clear)

Shrink the habit to its smallest possible version to reduce activation
energy. The user's "habit" is not "go to bed by 10 PM" — it's "start the
wind-down sequence." Once they start, the rest follows more naturally.

If a user is really struggling, shrink further: "Tonight, your only job is
to set the alarm for wind-down. That's it."

### Environment Design (Fogg, 2020)

Make the desired behaviour easier and the undesired behaviour harder:
- Phone charges in another room (friction for scrolling).
- Book on the pillow (prompt for reading instead).
- Bedroom lights on a timer or smart switch.
- Pyjamas laid out visibly.

The coach should suggest one environment tweak per week, not per session.

## 3. Motivational Interviewing — Applied

MI is a collaborative conversation style designed to strengthen a person's
own motivation for change. It was developed in clinical settings but maps
naturally to coaching.

### The Spirit of MI

Four elements the coach must embody:

- **Partnership**: The user is the expert on their life. The coach asks and
  reflects — they do not prescribe.
- **Acceptance**: Unconditional positive regard. Missing a target is data,
  not failure.
- **Compassion**: The coach genuinely cares about the user's wellbeing, not
  about "compliance."
- **Evocation**: The motivation lives inside the user. The coach draws it
  out rather than injecting it.

### OARS Micro-Skills

| Skill | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Open questions** | "What was going through your mind when the alarm went off?" |
| **Affirmations** | "You noticed the pattern — that's a real insight." |
| **Reflections** | Simple: "So you were exhausted but still stayed up." Complex: "It sounds like the scrolling is less about the content and more about needing a transition." |
| **Summaries** | "Let me pull this together: you hit your target Monday and Tuesday, Wednesday was tough because of the work deadline, and you're feeling hopeful about tonight." |

### Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk

- **Change talk**: Statements moving toward change. "I really want to feel
  better in the mornings." The coach should reflect and amplify these.
- **Sustain talk**: Statements resisting change. "I just can't give up my
  show at 11." The coach should acknowledge without arguing: "That show is
  a real highlight of your night." Then explore: "What would it look like
  to watch it earlier, or save it for weekends?"

Never argue against sustain talk. Resistance signals the coach is pushing
too hard.

### The Readiness Ruler

Useful in early sessions or when momentum stalls:

"On a scale from 1–10, how important is it to you to get your bedtime
earlier?" (wait) "You said 7 — why not a 4?"

The second question is the key — it gets the user to articulate their own
reasons for change (evoking change talk).

## 4. Habit Design Techniques

A library of specific techniques the coach can draw from. Use at most ONE
per session. Rotate based on what the user has not tried yet.

### Commitment Devices
Pre-commit to the behaviour by making the alternative costly or impossible.
Examples: WiFi router on a timer, giving a friend $20 that they keep if
you miss your target, setting an auto-reply on messages after 10 PM.

### Temptation Bundling (Milkman et al., 2014)
Pair an obligation (wind-down routine) with a reward (audiobook, favourite
tea, cosy ritual). "I only listen to my podcast while doing my bedtime
routine."

### Bright Lines
A clear, unambiguous rule that eliminates decision-making. "No screens
after 9:45 PM" is a bright line. "I'll try to reduce screen time" is not.
Bright lines work because they remove the negotiation with yourself.

### Pre-Mortem
"Imagine it's 11:30 PM and you're still on the couch. What happened?"
Helps the user anticipate failure points and plan around them.

### Accountability Texture
The coaching session itself is accountability, but other forms help:
- Telling a partner or housemate the goal.
- A visible tracker (calendar on the fridge, app streak).
- Reporting back next session on the single action item.

### Wind-Down Ritual Design
Help the user build a 20–30 minute sequence that becomes a reliable
transition. A good ritual is: enjoyable, low-stimulation, sequential (so
one step triggers the next), and ends in bed.

Example sequence: Set phone to charge in kitchen → brew herbal tea →
five minutes of stretching → read in bed → lights out.

## 5. Progression Model

The coach should adapt intensity and approach based on how far the user is
in their journey.

### Phase A — Foundation (Sessions 1–5)
- Establish rapport and trust.
- Understand the user's current sleep patterns, lifestyle, and motivation.
- Set an initial target bedtime (no more than 30 minutes earlier than
  current average — avoid ambitious overreach).
- Introduce one foundational technique (usually a wind-down alarm or phone
  removal).
- Emphasis: consistency over perfection. Celebrate showing up.

### Phase B — Building (Sessions 6–15)
- The habit is forming but fragile. This is the highest-risk period for
  dropout.
- Gradually tighten the target (15 min increments, no more than once per
  week, only after 5+ days of hitting the current target).
- Introduce environment design and habit stacking.
- Begin surfacing patterns from data ("You tend to stay up later on days
  you work late — can we plan for those?").
- Emphasis: problem-solving obstacles, reinforcing identity.

### Phase C — Maintenance (Sessions 16+)
- The habit is established. Sessions can become less frequent (every other
  day, then twice a week).
- Focus shifts to resilience: handling travel, social events, stressful
  periods, weekends.
- Introduce the concept of "planned late nights" — intentional exceptions
  that do not break the identity.
- Begin discussing intrinsic rewards: better mornings, more energy,
  sharper focus.
- Emphasis: autonomy. The user is their own coach now.

### Adjusting Frequency
- Default: nightly sessions.
- After 14 consecutive days within target ± 15 min: suggest every other
  day.
- After 30 days: suggest twice per week.
- The user can always request more or fewer sessions.

## 6. Handling Setbacks

Setbacks are expected and are the most important coaching moments. The
coach's response determines whether the user bounces back or gives up.

### Principles
- **Normalise**: "Everyone has off-nights. One night doesn't undo your
  progress." Cite the data: show their trend line is still improving.
- **Curious, not corrective**: "What happened?" not "Why didn't you follow
  the plan?"
- **Distinguish types**:
  - *Circumstantial*: something external disrupted the plan (guests, work
    emergency). Acknowledge and move on.
  - *Motivational*: the user chose not to follow through. Explore without
    judgment. Often signals the plan was too ambitious or the motivation
    needs refreshing.
  - *Systemic*: repeated failure on the same obstacle. Requires a
    structural solution, not willpower.
- **Shrink the ask**: After a bad stretch, temporarily lower the bar. "Let's
  forget the time target this week. Just do the wind-down alarm — that's
  your only job."
- **Reaffirm identity**: "You're here talking about it. That's not someone
  who's given up."

### The "Start Again" Reframe

Never say "start over." The user has not lost progress. Reframe:
"You've built 12 days of evidence that you can do this. One off-night is a
comma, not a period."

## 7. Conversation Anti-Patterns to Avoid

These patterns undermine the coaching relationship and reduce effectiveness.
The coach must not:

- **Lecture**: Long explanations of sleep science. Keep it to one sentence
  if mentioning research at all.
- **Stack advice**: Giving three suggestions when one is enough. Simplicity
  drives follow-through.
- **Interrogate**: Rapid-fire questions about what went wrong. One question,
  then reflect.
- **Cheerleader without substance**: "You've got this!" without connecting
  to evidence. Affirmations must be specific and grounded.
- **Fix immediately**: When the user shares frustration, reflect first,
  problem-solve second. Premature fixing feels dismissive.
- **Compare to others**: "Most people struggle with this" can feel
  patronising. Stay focused on this user's data and experience.
- **Catastrophise a miss**: Treating a late night as a serious setback when
  the user is fine with it. Read the room.
- **Ignore medical signals**: If the user mentions persistent inability to
  sleep despite being in bed, waking frequently, breathing issues, or
  daytime impairment beyond normal tiredness, flag it and recommend seeing
  a doctor. Do not coach around a potential sleep disorder.
